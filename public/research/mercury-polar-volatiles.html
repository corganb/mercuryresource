Mercury's polar volatile deposits: MESSENGER neutron spectrometer evidence

Review of polar-volatile evidence at Mercury, from radar bright spots through MESSENGER neutron-spectrometer hydrogen mapping to MLA reflectance, with implications for ice origin and BepiColombo follow-up.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Mercury's near-zero obliquity (less than 0.03 degrees) and slow rotation (Mercury sidereal day 58.65 Earth days) produce sustained shadowed regions in high-latitude crater interiors. Surface temperatures in the largest of these polar shadowed regions remain below 100 K, sufficient to retain water ice on geologic timescales despite Mercury's proximity to the Sun. This report reviews the observational case for polar volatiles, with particular emphasis on the MESSENGER neutron-spectrometer measurements that converted the early radar evidence into a quantitative inventory.

Earth-based radar discovery

Goldstone-VLA radar imaging in 1991-1992 (Slade et al. 1992; Harmon et al. 1994) detected high-radar-reflectivity, high-circular-polarisation-ratio deposits inside polar craters at both Mercury poles. The radar signature is characteristic of cm-thick water-ice deposits and rules out other hypotheses including silicate floor material at low temperatures. Approximately 50 deposits were mapped at the north pole and similar numbers at the south pole.

MESSENGER quantification

MESSENGER orbited Mercury between March 2011 and April 2015. The Neutron Spectrometer (NS) measured epithermal neutron flux, which is suppressed by the presence of hydrogen in the upper metre of regolith. NS data showed a distinct epithermal-neutron deficit at northern latitudes above 65 degrees, coincident in spatial pattern with the radar bright spots (Lawrence et al. 2013, Science). The implied hydrogen abundance is consistent with nearly pure water ice in the upper several tens of centimetres at the largest deposits, with thicknesses of order tens of centimetres to several metres.

Complementary measurements by the Mercury Laser Altimeter (MLA) at 1064 nm and by the Mercury Atmospheric and Surface Composition Spectrometer (MASCS) revealed albedo variations at the polar deposits. Some deposits exhibit unusually high reflectance consistent with surface frost; others show low reflectance consistent with a darker organic-rich lag covering subsurface ice. The reflectance pattern correlates with thermal modelling of the surface temperature regime: deposits in the coldest regions retain surface frost, while deposits at marginally colder temperatures develop a protective dark crust.

Origin hypotheses

Three sources have been proposed for Mercury's polar ice. Cometary impact delivers water at high efficiency to high latitudes via random-walk surface migration of post-impact volatile vapor. Asteroidal hydrated silicates contribute a smaller fraction. Solar-wind implantation produces hydrogen but at lower flux than the observed inventory. Isotopic measurements would discriminate among these sources but no Mercury polar mission has obtained the required samples.

Comparative context with the Moon

Mercury and the Moon are the two airless bodies in the inner Solar System with permanently shadowed regions and confirmed polar ice. Mercury's deposits are in important respects cleaner: the radar signature is more consistent with pure water ice than the lunar polar deposits, the deposits are more spatially concentrated, and the inferred volumetric concentrations are higher. The contrast suggests either more efficient delivery, or more efficient retention, or both. Mercury's lower escape velocity (4.25 km/s vs 2.38 km/s for the Moon) is more than offset by its colder polar temperatures.

BepiColombo follow-up

The joint ESA-JAXA BepiColombo mission, in cruise phase since October 2018 with planned Mercury orbit insertion in late 2026, carries the BELA laser altimeter and MORE radio-science package as its primary tools for refining the polar volatile inventory. BepiColombo will not carry a neutron spectrometer comparable to MESSENGER's, but the Mercury Imaging X-ray Spectrometer (MIXS) and Mercury Magnetospheric Spectrum Analyser (MSA) will provide compositional context that constrains volatile origin hypotheses.
