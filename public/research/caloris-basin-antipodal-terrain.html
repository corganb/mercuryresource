Caloris Basin and the antipodal weird terrain: impact dynamics and seismic propagation

Review of the Caloris Basin impact structure and its antipodal weird-terrain expression, with focus on Mariner 10 and MESSENGER mapping of the basin floor, the implied impactor dimensions, and the seismic-focusing model for the antipodal disturbed surface.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Caloris Basin is one of the largest preserved impact structures in the inner Solar System. Approximately 1,550 km in diameter, it dominates the topography of Mercury's eastern hemisphere and provides the most accessible Solar-System example of antipodal terrain disruption: the basin's far-side projection on Mercury hosts a region of ridged and grooved terrain whose origin is interpreted as seismic focusing of the impact-generated stress wave. This report reviews the basin and its antipodal companion as imaged by Mariner 10 and refined by MESSENGER.

Basin discovery and mapping

Caloris was discovered during the Mariner 10 first flyby in March 1974. Only the eastern half of the basin was illuminated during Mariner 10's flybys; the full basin extent was not directly imaged until MESSENGER's first flyby in January 2008. The combined Mariner 10 and MESSENGER imagery reveals a multi-ringed basin with a flooded floor that displays a unique pattern of radial extensional grabens (the Pantheon Fossae) at the centre - distinct from the more typical multi-ring lunar impact basin morphology.

Floor stratigraphy

The Caloris floor is composed of post-impact volcanic plains that buried the original impact-generated terrain. The plains are interpreted as flood basalts erupted within several hundred million years of the impact, with regional structural relief preserving original ring features in places. The plains' relatively low crater density (compared to the surrounding cratered terrain) places them in the late Tolstojan to early Mansurian periods - approximately 3.7 to 4.0 billion years old. The Pantheon Fossae, a star-burst pattern of grabens at the basin centre, are interpreted as a result of late-stage uplift and tensional fracturing as the basin's interior deformed during cooling and isostatic rebound.

Antipodal weird terrain

The antipodal point of Caloris - the location on Mercury directly opposite the basin - hosts a 500-km region of disturbed, hilly, lineated terrain that does not match the morphology of any other Mercury terrain. Mariner 10 imaged the region during its second flyby in 1974, and MESSENGER refined the morphology with sub-kilometre-resolution coverage during its orbital phase. The terrain consists of clusters of 5-10 km hills, broad ridges, and irregular grooves with no preferred orientation locally but with a global pattern centred on the antipodal point.

Seismic-focusing model

The standard interpretation, originally proposed by Schultz and Gault (1975), is that the Caloris impact generated a seismic stress wave that propagated through Mercury's interior and converged at the antipodal point. The convergence focused stress amplitudes sufficient to disrupt the surface to depths of several kilometres, producing the chaotic terrain pattern. The model successfully predicts the size and morphology of the antipodal region given the impact energy implied by the basin diameter.

Two refinements have been added in modern analyses. First, surface-coupled Rayleigh waves (which travel along the planet's surface rather than through its interior) contribute to the focusing as they converge on the antipode and likely dominate the surface-disruption process near the focal point. Second, the convergence sensitivity to interior structure - notably the size and state of Mercury's core - means that the antipodal-terrain area can be used to constrain interior-properties at the time of the impact, even though seismic-velocity profile changes since then complicate direct inversion.

Impact energy estimates

The basin diameter implies an impactor 100-200 km across, depending on assumed impactor density and impact velocity. The typical impact velocity at Mercury's heliocentric distance is in the range 30-50 km/s. The total impact energy is estimated at 10^28 to 10^29 J, comparable to the largest preserved Solar-System impacts (South Pole-Aitken Basin on the Moon, Hellas on Mars, Borealis on Mars, the proposed Caloris on Mercury, and a small number of comparably-sized features on the icy moons).

Outlook

BepiColombo, in cruise phase since October 2018 with Mercury orbital insertion planned for late 2026, will provide higher-resolution topography and gravity-anomaly data over the Caloris Basin. The Mercury Imaging X-ray Spectrometer (MIXS) will refine compositional mapping of the basin floor and antipodal region. Combined with stereo imaging from BepiColombo's two cameras, the data will sharpen estimates of basin depth and antipodal-terrain volume that constrain impact-dynamics models.
